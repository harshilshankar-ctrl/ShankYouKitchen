## Business structure (talk to an accountant — this is orientation, not legal advice)

- **Start:** Sole proprietorship or single-member LLC in Harshil's name. LLC (~$50–500 depending on state) gives liability protection, which matters for food.
- **Required before charging strangers:** check your state/county **cottage food law** vs. commercial kitchen requirement. In-home chef service (cooking in the client's kitchen) is often the *easier* regulatory path than delivery from a home kitchen. Verify: food handler's certificate (cheap, online, ~1 day), county health dept rules, and general liability insurance (~$300–600/yr, e.g. FLIP — Food Liability Insurance Program).
- **Money:** separate bank account from day one. Track every grocery receipt. Venmo/Zelle is fine for the sandbox; move to Square/Stripe invoicing once real.

## The two offerings

**1. The Weekly Table — $300–500/week** (price by household size)
One set chef's menu, no customization — that's the efficiency model that makes the price work. Rotating soup (vegetable, squash, root veg, loaded baked potato), composed salad (interesting greens + corn/artichokes/olives/fried shallots) with homemade dressing (EVOO, quality vinegar, Dijon, honey, garlic & onion), ~4 lbs protein (1 lb tiger shrimp, 1 lb steak, 1–2 lbs salmon/halibut/swordfish), a carb (couscous with veg & bacon), cut fruit.
**Delivery model:** ONE evening, ONE route, back-to-back 20-minute windows (e.g. Sunday 6:00 / 6:20 / 6:40 / 7:00 / 7:20 / 7:40). Cook once, drive one loop. Capped at 6 slots.
**Packaging:** glass containers for everything that stores; plastic deli containers for anything meant to be microwaved in-container. Every container gets a printed reheat card — reheating done wrong is the #1 way delivered food disappoints, so this is part of the product.

**2. The In-Home Chef — by quote**
Fully custom. Consultation first (lose / gain / eat better / just great food). Harshil shops; groceries billed at cost + service fee. Cleaning the kitchen afterward is included — say it every time, it closes deals. Positioning: not competing with $20 tray meals — competing with the client's own time.
**Future add-on:** a recommended "functioning kitchen" list for in-home clients (pans, knives, tools Harshil needs on site) — send it after the consultation so day one runs smooth.

## Pricing logic — $300–500/week

- Cost reality: protein alone runs ~$100 (3–4 lbs at $25–30/lb for tiger shrimp, steak, salmon/halibut/swordfish). Add produce, containers, reheat cards, 2–3 store shopping runs, cook time, one delivery route, and dishes afterward — the price is labor + customization, not just food cost.
- At $300, roughly $200 covers everything above food cost across a slot; at 6 slots that's a real test day. Batch efficiency (one menu, one route) is the entire margin model — never break it for a special request; that's what the In-Home service is for.
- In-Home: quote = groceries (at cost, receipts shown) + flat session fee. Suggested starting fee: $200–350/session depending on hours and menu complexity. Never quote before the consultation.
- Founding-client hook for Saturday: "founding six" lock in their week-one price for 8 weeks.

## Product principles — fridge-first (the moat)

1. **Only cook what survives day three.** A dish that's perfect fresh but dies in the fridge doesn't go on the menu. (Even Michelin food is sad from a fridge.) Soup is the hero for exactly this reason — foolproof reheating.
2. **If it's not extremely easy to consume, people won't eat it.** Stressed clients will write off $20 of food rather than think about it. Every item must be zero-thought: open, heat per card, eat.
3. **Everything gets sauce.** Fridge time steals moisture; sauce puts it back. No dry protein leaves the kitchen without its sauce packed alongside.
4. **Pack in parts.** Greens washed, dried, packed alone. Tomatoes, olives, wet components separate. Dressing in its own jar — it splits because there are no emulsifiers; "shake it" is on the card.
5. **Reheat cards are part of the product.** Once food leaves your hands it's a crapshoot — the card is how you keep control of quality (and of blame) after handoff.
6. **Smart sourcing.** 2–3 stores minimum per run; organic only where it matters. Central Valley proximity is a genuine story — freshest pick of every season — tell it in the pitch.

## Flexibility & retention — designing around food fatigue

The honest problem: even great fridge food gets old. Clients will hit week 4 and think "I'm paying $400 to eat cold food from the fridge." Design for that instead of pretending it won't happen:

1. **Portion slightly under.** Deliver a touch less than they think they want. They finish everything, feel good (no waste guilt — the fastest way to lose a client is food rotting in their fridge at $400/week), and they're hungry for next week. Craving is retention.
2. **Weekly opt-in, not opt-out.** Thursday text: "In for Sunday? Menu is X." One tap. Skipping a week is frictionless and judgment-free — a skipped week costs nothing; a guilty cancellation loses the client forever. People who skip and come back are your best customers.
3. **Expect a rhythm, not a subscription.** Model the business on clients taking 2–3 weeks a month, not 4. Price and slot math should work at ~70% weekly participation. Backfill skipped slots from a waitlist text.
4. **Fight fatigue with the menu, not with options.** The menu rotates hard week to week (soup and proteins change), and seasonal resets (squash season, stone fruit season) give people a reason to come back. Variety comes from the calendar, not customization.
5. **Say the quiet part in the pitch:** "Some weeks you'll want a restaurant instead. Skip. This is here to make your life easier, not to be another obligation."

## Test-run design (weeks 1–4)

- Cap at 6 families, one delivery day (add a second only if 6 fill fast).
- Every delivery includes a feedback text: "One thing to keep, one thing to change?"
- Track per-slot: grocery cost, hours, drive time. After 4 weeks you'll know real margin and whether $150–225 holds.
- Success criteria for continuing: 4+ retained families after week 4, and effective hourly rate you're happy with.

## Moving forward (after the sandbox)

1. Convert the best Weekly Table clients into In-Home consultations — it's the premium upsell and the more defensible business (customization is the moat; reheated food fatigue is real and you've already identified it).
2. Buy the domain, add photos of real food from the first weeks (nothing sells like your actual plates).
3. Simple referral: one free soup for every referred family that books.
4. Revisit structure at ~$2k/mo revenue: LLC if not done, bookkeeping, quarterly taxes.
